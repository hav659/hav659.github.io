CASE STUDY: MN DEPARTMENT OF HEALTH

The Existing Problem

For this case study, our team set out to redesign the mobile version of the Minnesota Department of Health's COVID-19 response page. The MN DOH desktop website does a reasonable job sharing information, but is also crowded with its wealth of information. This problem is made significantly worse when on mobile alongside poor mobile elements. Our team of three shared the work as researchers, designers, and testers to create a much more accessible product.

Link to Presentation

Research

User research included a number of interviews and a small survey. Interviews were aimed at people who had a variety of living situations and backgrounds. The primary focus of our research was discovering which information people found valuable and helpful as it relates to COVID-19, as the original website seemed to lack a priority amongst its information. Our research concluded that users wanted big-picture information without the politics. How bad are the current outbreaks? What is the progress on the vaccine?

Defining the Problem

After reviewing research, we created a proto-persona to help aid in defining our problem. Alongside a storyboard, this persona helped focus our efforts. We aimed to make the most important and prominent information easily accessible, while making more obscure or situational information easier to find for those who need it.

Design

Because our team was working within an established brand, we tried to stay as true to the established style as possible. Major changes were in iconography and headers streamline the flow of information. Our early wireframes put a major emphasis on cards containing easy-to-read icons.

Prototyping

The final prototypes took all previous research and design decisions and put them into a clean product. The critical, real-time information users expressed interest in seeing were made easy to access. Large, simple icons in blue take away any mystery as to where users are going. Recommending frequently asked questions in the search bar shortens the amount of time it takes users to find the information they critically need.

Next Steps

As COVID-19 is an ongoing virus with constantly-evolving situations, a well-designed website would require more agility to adapt to changing conditions. Live graphs and integrated Twitter feeds from medical officials are a few ways the site could improve.

Another direction the project could be taken is an overhaul to the desktop version of the site. Although not as poor as the mobile site, the desktop site overwhelmed many users with cluttered information. Connecting the two sites to create a responsive site would also be a major priority in continued development.
